“Freedom is not given, it is taken.” -Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose
Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose was one of the most prominent leaders of the Indian independence movement. He was a fierce freedom fighter who spent his life fighting for the freedom of India from British colonial rule. Bose was born on January 23, 1897, in Cuttack, Orissa, and was the ninth of fourteen children in his family. Bose was a brilliant student and excelled in academics and extra-curricular activities. He was deeply influenced by the teachings of Swami Vivekananda and joined the Ramakrishna Mission when he was just 16 years old. Bose went on to study law at the University of Calcutta and passed the Indian Civil Service examination in 1920. However, he resigned from the service soon after, citing his dissatisfaction with the British administration's treatment of Indians. Bose's political career began in 1921 when he joined the Indian National Congress. He became the General Secretary of the party's Bengal branch in 1923 and later the President of the Bengal Provincial Congress Committee in 1928. In 1930, Bose led a massive civil disobedience movement in Bengal against British rule. He was arrested and jailed for a year for his role in the movement. After his release from prison, he was elected as the Mayor of Calcutta in 1930. In 1938, Bose was elected the President of the Indian National Congress, defeating Mahatma Gandhi's preferred candidate, Pattabhi Sitaramayya. However, he resigned from the post a year later due to differences with the party's leadership. Bose believed that the Indian National Congress was not doing enough to fight for India's freedom and that peaceful protests were not enough to overthrow British colonial rule. He believed that the only way to free India from British rule was through armed struggle. During World War II, Bose sought the help of Axis powers to overthrow British rule in India. He went to Germany and Japan and sought their assistance in his freedom struggle. Bose formed the Indian National Army (INA) in Singapore with the help of the Japanese, which comprised of Indian soldiers who had been captured by the Japanese in Southeast Asia. The INA fought against the British in Burma and India and managed to gain control of parts of Northeast India. However, they were ultimately defeated by the British. Bose's sudden disappearance in August 1945 remains one of the most mysterious incidents in the history of India's independence movement. Some claim that he died in a plane crash in Taiwan while others believe that he survived the crash and went on to lead a secretive life in the Soviet Union. Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose remains an iconic figure in India's history and is widely regarded as a hero and a freedom fighter. His legacy is celebrated in India every year on his birthday, January 23, which is also observed as "Parakram Divas." Bose's Role in India's Freedom Struggle: Bose played a crucial role in India's freedom struggle. He was a fierce nationalist who believed that India should be free from British colonial rule at all costs. Bose's political ideology was heavily influenced by his time in Europe, where he was exposed to the ideas of socialism and nationalism. Bose believed that peaceful protests and non-violent resistance were not enough to overthrow British colonial rule in India. He believed that India needed a strong military force to fight for its freedom. This led him to form the Indian National Army (INA) with the help of the Japanese during World War II.
